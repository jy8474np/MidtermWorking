An Elementary Introduction

Sherlock Holmes is a fictional consulting detective created by author Sir Arthur Conan Doyle. Holmes debuted in A Study in Scarlet, which appeared in Mrs. Beeton's Christmas Annual in 1887. After being encouraged by his contemporary, Oscar Wilde, to write more Holmes adventures, Conan Doyle followed-up his initial story with The Sign of the Four. Before he knew it, Conan Doyle had a wildly popular and extremely profitable franchise on his hands.


The Gold Standard

Sherlock Holmes remains the ideal detective. His his methodical approach to investigation, his famous attention to detail, and his masterful command of deductive reasoning have enshrined him as the pinnacle of the crime solving profession. Indeed, Scotland Yard's information technology system is acronymed H.O.L.M.E.S.


Save Our Sherlock

Sherlock Holmes may be the first example of a Save Our Show campaign. After Conan Doyle killed off Holmes in 1893, he was bombarded with letters and telegrams imploring him to revive the character. Conan Doyle relented in 1901, releasing perhaps the most well-known Holmes novel, The Hound of the Baskervilles.


A Long Shadow

Sherlock Holmes' influence continues to pervade modern culture. Holmes appears in nearly every medium: television, movies, books, cartoons, comics, games; the list is endless. The very name "Sherlock" has become synonymous with genius ... or know-it-all-ism.





The iconic image of Sherlock Holmes features a style of hat he never wore and a style of pipe he never smoked.


On the Trail of Sherlock Holmes


  • Sherlock Holmes was created in 1887 by Edinburgh-born writer Sir Arthur Conan Doyle who believed that an astute audience should be able to solve mysteries along with their protagonist ... provided they were clever enough.

  • Holmes lived most of his adult life in London, famously residing at 221b Baker St. Holmes disliked leaving his rooms unless he had to; the exception being when an enticing case warranted the effort of venturing into the outside world.

  • In 1893 Sherlock Holmes died after plunging into the Reichenbach Falls, locked in the arms of his arch nemesis Dr. James Moriarty. Or so he would have had the world believe.

  • After years of serving his country, Holmes finally hung up his top hat (which he wore instead of the oft-portrayed deerstalker) in 1915 and retired to South Sussex. He spent his golden years keeping bees and studying their habits.
